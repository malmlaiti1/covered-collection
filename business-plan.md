# Covered Collection — Business Plan

## Executive summary

Covered Collection is a US iOS-led subscription rental for modest women's fashion. Three plans at $89/$129/$169 unlock a 3-, 5-, or 8-item monthly wardrobe of curated modest pieces (long sleeves, opaque fabrics, knee-and-below hems, prayer-friendly silhouettes, hijab pairings) tagged by a 5-axis "Modesty DNA" preference engine with a religious-occasion calendar (Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Christmas, Easter, LDS General Conference) baked into the planning experience. The market is large and structurally underserved: DinarStandard's *State of the Global Islamic Economy 2024/25* reports modest fashion at US$327B in 2023 and projects US$433B by 2028; Pew Research's January 3, 2018 report estimated 3.45M Muslims of all ages in the US in 2017, and Pew's 2023–24 Religious Landscape Study reports Muslims at 1.2% of US adults with 44% holding a college degree and 26% a postgraduate degree — materially higher than the US average. The only US modest-fashion rental subscription identified (Modest Magnolia) is opaque and undercapitalized. We beachhead in Dearborn, expand to a 6-city US footprint by month 24, and exit to a strategic acquirer in 5–7 years.

## Market opportunity

**Global modest fashion.** DinarStandard's 11th *State of the Global Islamic Economy Report* (2024/25) reports **modest fashion at US$327B in 2023, projected to US$433B by 2028** at a ~5.8% CAGR. Earlier DinarStandard editions reported Muslim apparel spend at US$295B in 2021. These figures represent core Muslim consumer apparel spend, not rental TAM specifically — modest fashion is notoriously difficult to bound because non-Muslim modest consumers (LDS, Orthodox Jewish, Pentecostal Christian, modest-curious secular) are not captured in the Islamic-economy figure. Adding LDS and Orthodox Jewish modest apparel could plausibly extend the figure 8–12%.

**US Muslim demographics.** Pew's January 3, 2018 short-read estimated **about 3.45 million Muslims of all ages living in the U.S. in 2017** with Muslims making up about 1.1% of the total US population. Pew's 2023–24 Religious Landscape Study (published February 2025, n=36,908) reports Muslims at **1.2% of US adults**, with **44% holding a college degree and 26% a postgraduate degree** (Pew's `pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/06/18/` summary). Pew's 2011 hijab survey (referenced in Wikipedia's *Islam in the United States*) found **36% of Muslim American women wear hijab whenever in public, 24% wear it most or some of the time, and 40% never wear it** — so ~60% self-identify as hijab-wearing at least sometimes. Applied to the ~1.7M adult Muslim women in the US, this is roughly **1.0M hijabi US women plus ~0.7M Muslim women who dress modestly without hijab** = a primary addressable population on the order of 1.7M.

**LDS modest segment.** Pew places LDS at ~1.6% of US adults; the LDS Church's own April 2025 statistical report lists 17,509,781 global members (Church News, April 5 2025). US LDS women aged 18–45 are estimated at **~1.3M** (analyst estimate: ~6.5M US LDS × ~51% female × ~40% in this cohort). Roolee (Logan UT) has third-party revenue estimates that vary materially: **LeadIQ estimates roughly $15M annual revenue as of October 2024; Zippia estimates a peak of about $19M in 2023–2024; RocketReach's $6.6M figure appears materially understated** relative to other aggregators. Sweet Salt Clothing, Mikarose (founded December 2005 by Michaella "Mika" Lawson, Orem UT), and Dainty Jewell's are direct comparables; Utah modest-boutique price points cluster at **$40–$90/dress** per Deseret News (quoting Love Olive Co founder Stephanie Tarnasky).

**Orthodox Jewish segment.** Pew's 2020 Jewish Americans study (`pewresearch.org/religion/2021/05/11/`) estimates ~10% of US Jews are Orthodox; with ~5.8M US Jewish adults, that's **~530K–700K Orthodox adults plus children**, and Yale SOM's Insights piece projects Orthodox share to grow from 12% to 29% of US Jews by 2063 due to a ~3.3 child fertility differential. Tznius requirements (skirts past the knee, sleeves past the elbow, collarbones covered) align cleanly with the Modesty DNA spec. **Mimu Maxi (Brooklyn/Crown Heights, founded 2014 by sisters-in-law Mimi Hecht and Mushky Notik, opened a permanent retail store July 2023 at the American Dream Mall, and announced closure in June 2024 per Wikipedia)** has left this segment without a digital-native brand. Kosher Casual founder Gary Swickley publicly says "**60%–70% of the company's sales come from the Christian market**" (Israel365News/JewishLink) — confirming that modest brands marketed to one faith routinely cross-sell to another.

**Modest-curious secular + conservative Christian.** Pinterest reported a **50% increase in searches for 'modest inspo' in 2021** per Rawshot's industry-stats compilation. We include this in SAM but do not size it rigorously.

## Differentiated value proposition

1. **Modesty DNA** — 5-axis preference engine functions as search filter, personalization signal, and styling guarantee. No competitor uses it.
2. **Religious-calendar planning** — push notifications timed to Ramadan, Eid, Rosh Hashanah, LDS Conference; "plan your Ramadan box" 4 weeks ahead. Rawshot's compiled industry stats: "*searches for 'modest fashion' increase by 500% during the month of Ramadan globally*."
3. **Hijab pairings** — every product carries 1–3 recommended hijab colors/textures.
4. **Prayer-friendly tagging** — opaque, loose, two-piece, easy-to-wudu — none of which RTR or Nuuly model.

## Business model and unit economics

**Pricing (anchored to RTR/Nuuly/Armoire/Haverdash).**

- **Core $89/mo:** 3 items, 1 shipment, swap once. *Anchors against Haverdash $59 (3 items) and Armoire's lowest tier $89 (4 items).*
- **Lux $129/mo:** 5 items, 1 shipment, swap once. *Anchors against Nuuly $98 (6 items) and RTR $129 (5 items, full closet, post Aug-2025 hike).* Bullseye plan.
- **Unlimited $169/mo:** 6 items per shipment, unlimited swaps. *Sits between Armoire $119 and Armoire unlimited $249.* Upsell hero.

Economics work because of (a) retention compounding contribution margin, and (b) the fact that 60–70% of subscribers do not swap weekly even when allowed.

**LTV**

- Mature monthly churn 6–8% (RTR historically 7–10%; religious-calendar lock-in pulls toward the lower end).
- **LTV = $30 / 0.07 = $429** at 7% churn; $600 at 5% churn (aspirational, retention-flywheel mature).

## Operations plan

**Inventory sourcing — three tracks**

1. **Direct wholesale** with modest-native brands (Haute Hijab, INAYAH, Aab, Annah Hariri, Verona Collection, Sweet Salt, Mikarose, Niswa Fashion). Standard DTC modest wholesale runs 40–55% of MSRP with Net-30 to Net-60. Start with $25K POs across 6 brands.
2. **Consignment** for premium pieces — pay only as items are rented; CapEx → OpEx. Pitch: "We are top-of-funnel for younger consumers."
3. **Resale/EOL** — every retired garment goes to a "Closet Sale" rail at 50–70% of retail (mirroring Nuuly Thrift, which Nuuly's Head of Product Sky Pollard publicly described as "seamless integration between renting, owning, and upcycling through Nuuly Thrift").

**Warehousing.** Lease 2,500–4,000 sq ft in **Dearborn or Livonia MI** ($14–$18/sqft/yr commercial in 2025). Internal SOP first. Move to **ShipBob** at ~300 active subs (Simpl Fulfillment's 2026 ShipBob breakdown: $275/mo minimum, $975 setup, ~$0.30/pick, 15–30% postage markup). Stord is enterprise-priced (Red Stag's comparison) — avoid until you cross mid-market.

**Garment cleaning.** Master agreement with **Tide Cleaners** (P&G's national network, PERC-free) or **Zips** at $4.50–$6.50/garment for 500+/wk volume. Disclose "PERC-free" prominently — RTR's own clothing-subscription FAQ: "*We do not use (and have never used) halogenated cleaning solvents such as Perchloroethylene (PERC). After our thorough cleaning processes, most garments pass through a steam tunnel between 248°F and 302°F.*"

**Returns logistics.** Pre-paid USPS Ground Advantage commercial label in every box. On Shippo inbound-scan webhook, auto-transition items to `cleaning` and SMS customer ("We got your box! Build your next one.").

**Photography.** In-house from week 17. Local Dearborn hijabi photographer at $400–$600/day, 3 days/month. **Never use brand-supplied imagery** — it often shows the garment styled non-modestly and defeats the value prop.

**Customer service.** Front inbox tied to IG/email/WhatsApp; 1 part-time agent + community manager from week 17. Auto-replies in English and Arabic.

## Halal / Shariah considerations

The model is well-aligned with Shariah:

- **No riba** — subscription fee is fixed periodic rent (ujrah).
- **Acceptable gharar** — AAOIFI's Ijarah standard permits leasing usufruct with specified consideration. Avoid clauses that automatically transfer ownership at end of term (pushes toward Ijarah Muntahia Bittamleek with additional requirements).
- **No haram product categories** — curation discipline excludes anything immodest; brand premise.
- **Charitable component** — 2.5% of net profit (a clean nisab-aligned figure) to Zakat-eligible beneficiaries: **IRUSA / Islamic Relief USA, Helping Hand for Relief and Development, ICNA Relief, LaunchGood** emergency campaigns. Build into the brand day one; disclose on About page.

**On formal AAOIFI certification.** Not necessary at MVP. Recommendation: convene a **two-person Shariah Advisory Board at Series A**:

1. One US-based Muslim finance scholar — e.g., **Mufti Faraz Adam** (Amanah Finance scholar) or **Sheikh Joe Bradford** (Texas-based finance specialist with mass appeal).
2. One female community-facing scholar — e.g., **Dr. Tamara Gray of Rabata** or **Sheikha Iesha Prime**.

Pay $5–10K/yr advisory retainers. Issue an "ethical principles" page on the website rather than a formal compliance certificate at first — formal certification is expected of financial-services firms, not consumer apparel.

## Legal/regulatory

**Entity structure.** Form as **Delaware C-corp**. Reason: any future institutional equity round requires it, and converting an LLC to a C-corp later triggers tax friction. Founder can stay a Plymouth MI resident with a Delaware C-corp registered as a foreign entity to do business in Michigan (and later CA, NY, TX).

**Payment processing.** Stripe explicitly supports clothing-rental subscriptions; the risk vectors per Chargeback Stop and Payment Verdict are (a) chargeback ratio above ~0.65–0.75%, (b) inaccurate business descriptors, (c) ambiguous cancellation. Mitigations: clear billing descriptors ("COVCOL*RENTAL"), pre-charge email reminders 3 days before each renewal, one-tap in-app cancellation (also required by the FTC's 2024 "click-to-cancel" rule whose customer-experience expectation is now set regardless of court status).

**State sales tax on rentals.** *High-friction area.* Sales tax on garment rental varies by state — clothing is broadly tax-exempt in some states for purchases (NJ, PA, MN, VT) but rental services are typically taxable under "personal property rental" rules. Michigan generally taxes clothing rentals at the 6% state rate; California taxes rentals at the applicable district rate (7.25–10.25%); NY taxes rentals as services. Register in your launch state day one; use **Stripe Tax** or **Avalara** to automate; expand registrations as you cross post-Wayfair economic-nexus thresholds (typically $100K in sales or 200 transactions/state). Budget $5–10K/yr for a sales-tax SaaS once you cross 10 states.

**Import duties.** Sourcing from US, UK, Turkey, UAE, Indonesia. Apparel HTS codes carry 8–17% duties depending on fabric. Budget 12% as planning average.

|---|---|---|---|
| Inventory damage/loss | High (~3% std) | Medium | Built into COGS; charge card-on-file for damage > "general wear"; mirror RTR's tiered damage fees |
| Theft / non-return | Low–Med | Medium | Card-on-file authorization at signup; auto-charge retail after Day 45; small fraud model from Day 1 |
| Religious/cultural backlash ("commercializing hijab") | Medium | High | Muslim founders/team voices lead; 2.5% net profit to Zakat-eligible orgs; Shariah advisory board at Series A; never use modesty as marketing gimmick (Melanie Elturk in Fashionista: "*there was this legitimate fear of combining capitalism with a religious garment*") |
| Economic downturn | Medium | High | $89 entry tier is recession-friendly; subscription pause feature; Haverdash's $59 demonstrates demand at lower price |
| Single-brand supply dependency | Med | High | Cap any single brand at 12% of inventory; 30-day flex consignment |
| Stripe shutdown | Low | Catastrophic | Parallel **Adyen** or **Worldpay** processor ready; keep chargebacks <0.5%; never run trials with un-pre-charged cards |
| App Store rejection | Low | High | Apple guideline 3.1.3(e) permits external billing for physical-goods rentals; Sign in with Apple required; pre-submit Review notes explaining religious calendar |
| Photography pipeline backlog | Medium | Medium | In-house photographer + 90-day content calendar planned a quarter ahead |

## Brand & positioning

**Name analysis — "Covered Collection" vs. "Layla & Co."**

- **Covered Collection** is faith-neutral, accessible to LDS/Orthodox Jewish/Christian, and easier to defend in trademark search. **Recommend keeping.**
- **Layla & Co.** signals more clearly to the Muslim community but limits LDS/Christian extensibility. There are multiple live Layla-formative marks in IC 025 on USPTO's TESS.

**Action:** Conduct a TESS search (uspto.gov/trademarks-application-process/search-trademark-database) for "Covered Collection" in **IC 025** (clothing) and **IC 035** (retail/rental services) before formal launch; file **1(b) intent-to-use** applications in both classes. Budget $1,500 in filing fees + $2,500 in attorney time.

**Tagline options.**

1. "**Get dressed without compromise.**" (universalizes)
2. "**Your closet, your calendar, your covered.**" (signals the calendar)
3. "**Style that respects your story.**" (modest-curious-friendly)
4. "**Curated for the way you cover.**" (faith-aware, owns "cover") ← **primary**
5. "**Rental, refined. Modesty, redefined.**" (positioning hero)

Use **#4** as primary, **#1** for broader campaigns.

**Brand voice.** Warm, confident, never preachy. Bilingual where authentic (Arabic transliterations where natural, never tokenized).

**Aesthetic.** Editorial neutrals (cream, terracotta, deep moss, navy) with editorial photography that puts the woman first, garment second. Never use the word "haram"; never lead with religious obligation; lead with style and ease.
